The Four Pillars

Every systematic exam moves through these four actions, in order.

LOOK

Inspection

Visual examination for any abnormalities.

FEEL

Palpation

Use the hands to feel for tenderness, masses, or pulsations.

TAP

Percussion

Tap the body to tell whether tissue beneath is air-filled, fluid-filled, or solid.

LISTEN

Auscultation

Use a stethoscope to hear internal sounds.

The Pulmonary Exam

Anatomical lines localize every finding. The assessment follows the four pillars.

Bony landmarks

ClavicleScapulaVertebral processes
  1. Observation. Watch rate, rhythm, and effort. Flag accessory-muscle use (neck or abdominal), blue lips, or leaning forward.
  2. Palpation. Check for tenderness or asymmetry. For chest expansion, place thumbs lateral to the vertebral line and watch them move apart on a deep breath.
  3. Percussion. Strike the left finger with the tip of the right middle finger using a sharp, relaxed wrist. Dullness can mean fluid or consolidation.
  4. Auscultation. Listen to 14 positions on the back and sides plus anterior points. Compare bilaterally, and never listen through clothing.

Cardiac Auscultation

Four listening areas on the chest. Tap a marker to place your stethoscope.

Base (2nd ICS) Apex (4th–5th ICS)
Auscultation area

Aortic Area

Right 2nd intercostal space

The upper-right listening post. Sits over where the aorta leaves the heart — one of the two "base" points at the 2nd ICS.

Tap any of the four markers on the chest to move your stethoscope.

The two sounds

S1 & S2 — "lub-dub"

Each beat is two valve-closure sounds. Press a button to hear the rhythm play out.

lubdub
S1 — closure of the tricuspid & mitral valves (start of systole)
S2 — closure of the pulmonic & aortic valves (start of diastole)

Peripheral Pulses

Assess amplitude, rate, rhythm, and symmetry. Tap a point on the body to learn where to press.

Pulse point

Carotid

Neck

The strongest central pulse. Critical safety rule: never press both carotids at the same time — you can compromise blood flow to the brain.

Amplitude describes pulse strength: bounding, weak, or thready.
